An Ode to Capitalism

Do I have anything important to say? About anything? Am I slowly becoming one of those people that don't have any real thoughts or opinions on anything? Someone who has a list of stock phrases whenever someone asks them about their view on things? Or am I slowly becoming more entrenched in the realities of becoming an adult in a developed country? Where it is difficult to find enough time for yourself to read and think about things so that you have a deeper understanding of them.

I replayed Ted Chiang's interview with Ezra Klein on the Ezra Klein Show and he said something to the effect that it's not AI or the future of tech that worries him but the extent to which the tech industry is embedded in capitalism . It echoes Blake Lemoine's, the software engineer who got suspended by Google for publicly airing out his concerns about Google's AI project LaMBDA becoming sentient, claim that it is not that there are bad people at Google but that Google as a corporation exists in an environment where business interests (collection data on its users, maximizing revenue, buying out competition etc.) are prioritized over ethical considerations.

If the tech world is the last refuge of hope for the future, it is because technology has become so integrated with our lives that it is becoming impossible to imagine our lives without it. We don't need our phones and laptops to live as human beings. It is just that the demands of living in a post-industrial economy has turned that which was once a convenience - quick and easy access to information - into a necessity.

I have no issue with this since no one is forcing me to live like a normal person. If I really wanted to I could opt out from living in modern society and learn survival skills so I can live on my own in the woods. The issue is that life even in a developed country with an advanced economy is unbearably stressful and unfulfilling. Unless you have some real friends or relationships with whom you can share ideas or talk to about your problems or things that you really care about; or some strong sense of purpose or a set of values that you can build your life around; or a hobby or passion that you can cultivate toward something fruitful, your life will inevitably become a monotonous routine of waking up and getting ready for work.

The worst part is that I have a strong feeling that I am compromising some of my core values. It's not that I don't think people should not work and take care of themselves. It's just that I am in a position where I have to find a way to get into job that pays me more so that I can have a better future ahead of me. While there certainly is a part of me that really wants that, I can't help but feel that just by thinking about my future in those terms means that I have pretty much sold out to capitalism and that I am a willing participant in an economic system that claims to be egalitarian but isn't.

Los Angeles is a beautiful place to live. But it is also place where the extreme pros and cons of capitalism converge: a lot of job opportunities, access to high end consumer goods and services, nice gentrified neighborhoods and clean beaches but also high cost of living, one of the highest homeless populations in the world, inadequate public transportation and (arguably) no social safety net.

There is an appeal to living in a highly capitalistic society in that you think that you can make it if you just work hard enough. When you do you get to buy property and travel around the world and do all those things that purportedly constitute a meaningful and fulfilling life. But if you don't, you end up becoming one of those embittered people that curse their luck and find some story to tell themselves to assuage their sense of personal failure.

Living in the U.S. for the past several months keeps bringing me back to that line from The Wire: "the game is rigged, but you cannot lose if you do not play."

I do not want to lose and I do not want to play. But I need to pay my rent.
