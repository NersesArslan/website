Ludwig Wittgenstein

If someone were to ask me why I was obsessed with Wittgenstein and his philosophy when I was in college I would not be able to give them an answer off the top of my head. I've come to appreciate that I've developed a special relationship with Wittgenstein that I sometimes have trouble explaining to myself. I've admired other writers and intellectuals - David Foster Wallace, Roberto Bola√±o, Noam Chomsky and Fyodor Dostoevsky - but Ludwig Wittgenstein was special. It was not just his philosophy and the kind of questions his method of philosophy was raising. It was something about his character.

As an introvert I had a very active and stimulating inner life that was difficult to share with others. I struggled to express my thoughts because I noticed that there was a huge gap between what I was thinking and what I was trying to say. The issue wasn't that I wasn't able to articulate these thoughts in language, I had a pretty good idea of what I wanted to express when I wrote it down on paper with time to think and revise my drafts. But when it came to expressing myself in front of people, classmates, friends, family or anyone with whom I tried having a deep conversation with, I struggled to express myself.

A lot of it is nerves, but I would also like to think that it was a lack of experience in knowing how to talk with other people. The written word was my refuge for making sense of the world. But there were times when I couldn't make sense myself. Ever since I started reading books as a child I had this strange idea that, with practice, I could perfectly express every thought that would ever come across my mind. With time and plenty of practice, I started becoming a better writer. My talent showed itself in my English class and my habit to scribble stories on my notebooks and laptop.

Some time around college I found myself being better able to express myself around people. I still struggled with awkwardness and being able to gauge a flow of conversation, but around certain people, friends and professors, I started being able to express myself in ways that I was previously not able to. I was able to think and speak clearly. What's more, in certain instances, I felt like I was able to command respect from others with what I said and how I said it. I started to have a growing appreciation for the spoken word.

What drew me to Wittgenstein and his philosophy were his observations about language. Specifically with how he approached philosophical problems in a novel way, by focusing language, the medium though which philosophical problems are articulated rather than the philosophical problems themselves. Of course I was also drawn by his sparse, cryptic and even sometimes lyrical sentences in the Tractactus.

The limits of my language are the limits of my world.

I also liked some of his mystical and quasi religious statements:

Death is not an event in life: we do not live to experience death. If we take eternity to mean not infinite temporal duration but timelessness, then eternal life belongs to those who live in the present. Our life has no end in the way in which our visual field has no limits.

As someone who was committed to pursue a career in philosophy, I took philosophical problems seriously. I thought that that one of the most serious problems was finding out how to live a meaningful life in the modern world. I thought that there were problems in how people lived in their day to day life, going to work and coming home, without giving much thought to important things like politics, climate change, what it means to be good, and how we reduce rising levels of inequality.

I think it was how true he was to himself and his philosophy. The fact that he never pursued a career in academia (as a matter of fact he seemed to abhor the trappings of academic life). The fact that he signed up to the front in WWI despite coming from one of the richest families in Europe. The fact that he gave away his inheritance to his sisters. The fact that he lived an ascetic life pursuing what he was passionate about.

I was deeply concerned with how little thought most people around me gave to the problem that I spent most of my time thinking about. I even struggled with existential depression and anxiety, and often turned to reading philosophy for solace. Reading Wittgenstein's philosophy and about his life was the closest I felt to finding answers to some of those problems. His thinking cut through a lot of academic bullshit formality and touched on deep questions, not just one the philosophical problems that pre-occupied me, but the spiritual and existential problems that I struggled to cope with.

Even if I never go back to studying philosophy and pursue a career in academia or teaching, I feel deeply indebted to him and his writings.

Thank you Ludwig.
