I started reading Tony Judt's Postwar a few weeks into Russia's invasion of Ukraine. I have been wanting to read the book for a long time but couldn't make time for it. Something about the invasion compelled me to get out of my way to make time to reading this 800+ page tome about Europe's Post-war history.

I never went out of my way to read non-fiction. But I started making a habit to have a non-fiction book laying around my pile a few years ago. I thought it would be a waste to spend all my years reading fiction without making an effort to read non-fiction. After all, there are few people out there who have the time or the inclination to sit down and read a book. How wasteful would it be as a lifelong reader to make it a point to only read fiction when I have the habit and temperament to read just about anything if only I made the time for it?

I value this habit of reading because it gives me privileged access to an author's mind that I cannot have when looking up results in a search engine or a Wikipedia page. It's not just that Judt is laying out a detailed account of the historical events, but he is providing context and commentary to certain events that adds a certain kind of substance. It's the kind of historical interpretation and commentary that gives you insight as to why certain things happened the way they did.

What really surprises me when I read history books like Judt's Postwar is how caricatured and sanitized our idea of certain historical events are. I thought that Germany's division into East and West was planned form the start. That the Allied and Soviet zones in Berlin were set up as soon as the dust settled off of WWII and that it was part of some sort of agreement between the western Allies and the Soviet Union. According to Judt, this split between West and East Germany and Berlin was a result of a series of miscalculations and misunderstandings between the U.S. and U.S.S.R. It was not at all planned.

Another thing that threw me off guard was how bad Eastern Europeans had it under Communism (especially Eastern European Jews). I always had this intuitive sense that Eastern Europe was under developed and 'backwards' in many respects, but I had no understanding of why this was the case. I didn't know why Western Europe was so developed and prosperous either. I just took it for granted that there was something about the way the governments and economies were set up. But I had no idea how much the success of the postwar years in Western Europe were because of circumstance beyond each nations' control. I also had very little sense of the devastation and the hardships that many people across the continent had to face for several years after the war. I was both horrified and in awe of what people in different points in history have to go through just to survive. It helps put things in perspective.

Did the book answer any questions about the current Russia-Ukraine invasion? Did it give me an understanding of why Putin would risk so much to invade another country? Not really. Well, at least not yet. I have not yet finished the book...

Postwar made me realize how much our sense of history is distorted by propaganda or a very sanitized high-school-textbook-version of events when what actually happened was far more complicated.

This also makes me want to ask the question: what are the consequences if most people around the world don't have a clear or coherent understanding of the historical events that shape and continue to shape the underlying structures of their present social, economic and political realities? How hard can it be for demagogues like Trump to sell an idea of the past if most people don't have clear idea of what actually happened?

I guess this goes with the territory of not having enough time to sit down and read because of the constraints of having to work; and that most people these days would rather spend their time distracting themselves with their phones or laptop instead of sitting alone in a room reading.

Is it just a matter of it being better to live in ignorance? That it's easier to accept our present realities by not investigating the chain of events that shape how things are now? I don't know.

The greatest lesson I may have learned from history is how certain forms of nationalism always crops up when a country is faced with economic decline. That politicians aren't necessarily horrible human beings, but shrewd tacticians playing the long-game for the greater good of their nation. That economic considerations always factor into political decisions (and vice-versa). That war and conflict are inevitable.

History is a nightmare from which we must awake.
