An Ocean of Time

I wonder if it’s possible to write without ego. I remember writing stories when I was a teenager whenever I had free time. I had just started reading for fun and was obsessed with using new words to craft sentences. Even if they didn’t amount to a story or a full paragraph. I had a curiosity and insatiable hunger to read and write. I had a very strong imagination that was also fed by the books that I’d spend most of my free time reading.

I wrote a few stories, one that I remember publishing in my high school newsletter. It as well received and a lot of people were impressed by it. It made me feel special in a way that getting good grades could not. I remember a girl in my bus telling me how great a writer I was. We started talking more and I eventually became infatuated with her. My writing and command of the English language commanded respect. I was held in high esteem, not just by my English teacher, but by my classmates, my parents and friends. I had built an identity around this one thing that I was good at. I would later focus more of my time studying to get good grades so that I can get accepted at a good college. I excelled in math and physics and the other sciences. I had built an identity and based my self-worth on my ability to be good at math and the sciences. I had built an identity out of being a good and well-mannered student. I was a young promising teenager with the whole world ahead of him.

That was the memory that persists of my teenage year up until high school. I always remember the good stuff and forget about the bad stuff. Like my parents separating, my shyness, my inability to speak good enough Arabic to get along with most people in my own country, my nascent depression and my all too normal insecurities. I didn’t know it at the time but I had it great. Even with my parents separation, they continued to provide my sister and me with a lot of financial and emotional support. They gave me the freedom to do whatever I wanted to do and to think for myself. They gave me freedom to choose whatever career I wanted to pursue instead of pushing me into engineering or medicine. They gave me everything I would need to flourish as an adult.

I don’t know why I did not pursue a career in writing. I had the knack for it. I definitely had or still have the ego, the need to say something and the curiosity to learn about new things. I had a very studious way going about my life. Always going from one point to another examining the best course of action. Too cautious to take risks but prudent enough to avoid mistakes. Until my ego got the better of me and devoured me from within while I was trying to put on the appearance of living a respectable life.

I didn’t write because I thought my studies were more important than my fanciful dreams of becoming a writer. I eventually replaced that dream with wanting to become an academic because there was still something romantic and noble about it. To be an intellectual that spent his time thinking and writing about important matters in research journals and molding young students into independent thinkers. There was a touch of ego about it that I never came to terms with. A pride from just doing what I really wanted and thought would be best for me. Also, a fear of failure.
